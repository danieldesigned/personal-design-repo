Fiat Team context
What the Fiat team owns, how we operate, and what success looks like.
What the Fiat team does
The Fiat team owns every touchpoint where users convert between traditional currency and crypto — and back. This includes buy/sell flows, bank deposits, card payments, local payment methods, fiat withdrawal, off-ramp integrations (like Paymonade), and the KYC/verification gates that unlock higher limits. We are the entry point to Binance for new users and a critical revenue surface for the company.
Core product surfaces we own
  • Buy Crypto — card (Visa/MC), bank transfer, third-party providers (Simplex, Banxa), local payment methods, P2P trading
  • Sell Crypto — direct to card (Paymonade), bank transfer, fiat wallet, P2P
  • Deposit Fiat — bank transfer, SEPA, SWIFT, local rails
  • Withdraw Fiat — bank withdrawal, card off-ramp, currency conversion
  • KYC & Identity — verification gates, limit management, enhanced due diligence (EDD)
  • Payment Method Management — adding/removing cards, bank account linking, payment limits & tiers
  • Binance Connect — fiat-to-crypto for third-party Web3 projects
Key stakeholders we design with
  • Fiat Product Managers (roadmap, OKRs, requirements)
  • Compliance & Legal (regulatory constraints, market-specific rules)
  • Engineering (feasibility, component reuse, handoff)
  • UX Research (usability studies, user interviews)
  • Risk & Fraud (friction calibration in payment flows)
  • Customer Support (identifying recurring pain points)

  • Audit all existing Fiat flows end-to-end: Buy, Sell, Deposit, Withdraw, KYC
  • Map the full Fiat user journey and identify where the biggest drop-offs occur
  • Interview 3–5 team members: PM, Engineering, Customer Support, Compliance, a peer designer
  • Review the last 3 shipped features — what was the outcome? What was cut and why?
  • Get fully onboarded on the Binance Flex design system and component library in Figma
  • Sit in on customer support sessions or read a week's worth of fiat-related tickets
  • Read any available research reports or usability test findings from the past 6 months

  • Own at least one feature or improvement end-to-end — from problem framing to handoff
  • Run a small usability test (even 3–5 sessions) and present findings to the team
  • Identify 3 quick wins in existing flows — high-friction moments that can be improved now
  • Document a design decision that wasn't previously written down (helps the whole team)
  • Establish your working rhythm with your PM — how often do you sync? How is work prioritized?
Days 61–90 — Lead
  • Present a design strategy proposal for a key Fiat initiative to stakeholders
  • Establish or improve the design review cadence with your team
  • Begin contributing back to the design system — propose or build a missing component
  • Build cross-functional relationships with Compliance, Risk, and Data — these are your power multipliers on Fiat

Design System
Design principles
Binance product development is guided by five core principles. Every design decision should be traceable back to at least one of these.
Simplicity
Crypto is complex. Our job is to abstract complexity, not expose it. Every screen should do one thing well. Remove anything that doesn't serve the user's primary task.
Principle 1
Trust
Users are moving real money. Design must convey security at every step — through clear status communication, transparent fee display, and consistent visual language.
Principle 2
Transparency
Fees, rates, processing times, and limits must be shown up front. Never hide costs or bury critical information. Users should never feel surprised after a transaction.
Principle 3
Innovation
Push the boundaries of what fiat onramp/offramp experiences look like. Benchmark against fintech leaders, not just crypto competitors.
Principle 4
Community
Designs must serve a global audience across very different financial contexts — from banked users in the EU to mobile-first users in emerging markets.
Principle 5
Atomic Design structure
Atoms
Smallest indivisible elements: color tokens, typography scale, spacing values, icons, individual input fields, buttons.
Level 1
Molecules
Groups of atoms: form field + label + error message, button + icon, card with price + currency tag.
Level 2
Organisms
Complex UI sections: payment method selector, amount input with rate display, KYC verification step, full form panel.
Level 3
Templates
Page-level layout scaffolding without real content: the buy flow shell, deposit screen template, confirmation layout.
Level 4
Pages
Templates filled with real content and data. These are the final deliverables your engineers build from.
Level 5

Visual design language
Dark-first, yellow-accented
Binance UI is primarily dark (surface: #181A20, background: #0B0E11). Yellow (#F0B90B) is used exclusively for primary CTAs, active states, and brand moments. Green (#03C087) signals success and navigational links. Red (#F6465D) is for errors, warnings, and sell actions.
Content-forward design
The interface should not compete with the content. Use subtle visual hierarchy — not decorative elements — to guide attention. Subtlety is the operating principle: let prices, rates, and status be the visual heroes.
Light and dark modes
All components must be designed for both light and dark mode from the start. Dark is the default; light is increasingly important for institutional and web-facing products. Never design one and adapt the other — co-design them using the Binance Flex token system.

Fiat-specific typography rules
  • Always use tabular numerals for amounts, rates, and countdowns — monospaced digits prevent layout jumps
  • Currency symbols should be slightly smaller than the amount (≈80% size) to create visual balance
  • Localized number formatting must be respected — 1,234.56 (EN) vs 1.234,56 (DE)
  • Legal/compliance text must meet minimum 11px size and pass WCAG AA contrast ratios
Design System
Component library
The Binance Flex component library in Figma. Before building anything custom, verify the component doesn't already exist.
Buttons & CTAs ▼
  • Primary: Yellow fill, black text — reserved for the single most important action per screen (Buy, Continue, Confirm)
  • Secondary: Ghost/outlined — supporting actions (View Details, Learn More)
  • Destructive: Red text or fill — Cancel, Delete, Reject
  • Full-width CTAs are standard in mobile flows. Never stack two primary buttons.
  • Sticky bottom CTAs keep Buy/Sell buttons accessible while browsing — do not paginate behind scroll
Input fields ▼
  • Amount inputs: large font, numeric keyboard triggered on mobile, inline currency toggle
  • Text inputs: 48px height on mobile (touch target), 40px on desktop
  • Always show real-time validation — don't wait for form submission
  • Error states: red border + below-field error message. Never red text inside the field.
  • For fiat amounts: always show the converted crypto equivalent in real-time below the input
Status indicators & feedback ▼
  • Processing states require animated indicators — never static spinners
  • Success screens: green checkmark + summary of what happened + primary next action
  • Error screens: clear explanation + what the user can do next — never just an error code
  • Pending states for bank transfers must show estimated completion time
  • Toast notifications for background events (payment received, order filled)
Cards & list items ▼
  • Payment method cards: icon + method name + masked details + status badge
  • Transaction history items: amount (primary), date/time, status pill, expandable for details
  • Use dividers between list items, not card borders for dense lists
  • Swipe actions on mobile (delete, set default) must have visual affordance
Modals & bottom sheets ▼
  • Mobile: use bottom sheets for confirmations and detail views — not full-screen modals
  • Desktop: centered modals for confirmations, side panels for details
  • Always include a dismiss handle/button — never trap the user
  • Confirmation modals for irreversible actions must restate the exact amounts involved
Forms — critical for Fiat ▼
  • Use correct HTML input types: tel for phone, number for amounts, email — triggers the right keyboard on mobile
  • Validate on blur (when leaving a field), not on keypress — avoid overwhelming the user mid-typing
  • Auto-format card numbers as the user types: 4444 4444 4444 4444
  • Numeric keyboards for all amount inputs — never show a text keyboard for money entry
  • Show password / card number reveal toggles — don't force masking on entry
  • Inline validation — error messages appear directly below the field, never in a toast
Loading states ▼
  • Prefer skeleton screens over spinners for layout-heavy pages — they set expectations and reduce perceived wait
  • Show progress for long operations: bank verification can take 30s+; never leave users on a blank screen
  • For bank transfers processing: show a step indicator ("Contacting your bank… Verifying… Almost done")
  • Rate refresh: show a live countdown timer (e.g. 29s remaining) — not just a spinner
  • Never use a static spinner for anything that takes more than 3 seconds

End-to-end design process
How projects move from a problem statement to a shipped, measured feature on the Fiat team.
1
Discovery & problem framing
Collaborate with PM and Research to understand the problem space. Review data, customer feedback, support tickets, and qualitative research. Define success metrics before touching design tools.
User interviews Data analysis Competitive audit Problem statement
2
Define & align
Create a design brief documenting the user problem, scope, constraints (regulatory, technical, timeline), and metrics. Get PM, Compliance, and Engineering aligned before moving to ideation.
Design brief Constraints mapping Stakeholder sign-off
3
Ideation & exploration
Low-fidelity exploration in Figma. Multiple concepts. Use FigJam for collaborative mapping of user flows. Share early and gather feedback — avoid designing in isolation.
User flows Wireframes Concept sketches Design critique
4
High-fidelity design
Build out the preferred concept in Figma using Binance Flex components. Design for all states: empty, loading, error, success, edge cases. Design for iOS, Android, and desktop concurrently — they share patterns but have platform-specific interactions.
Figma components All states Dark + light mode 3 platforms
5
Prototype & test
Build interactive prototypes in Figma for usability testing. Work with UX Research to run moderated or unmoderated sessions. Prioritize testing on the Fiat team's most critical flows: buy flow, deposit, and KYC.
Figma prototype Usability testing A/B testing setup
6
Dev handoff
Annotate designs in Figma with interaction notes, component specs, and edge case documentation. Hold a handoff meeting with engineering. Stay available for implementation Q&A and conduct design QA on builds.
Figma annotations Handoff meeting Design QA
7
Launch & measure
Monitor the feature post-launch using agreed success metrics. Run A/B tests where applicable. Document learnings in Confluence. Feed insights back into the next iteration.
Metrics review A/B analysis Iteration plan

Dev handoff standards
What engineering needs from design to ship accurately and efficiently.
Figma file organization
  • One Figma file per major feature/initiative. Archive previous versions, never delete.
  • Pages: Cover → Flows → Components → States & Edge Cases → Archive
  • Use the Binance Flex component library (shared Figma library) — never detach components without a reason documented in the annotation
  • Name frames clearly: [Platform] / [Flow] / [Screen name] / [State]
What to annotate
  • Micro-interactions and animation specs (duration, easing)
  • Component behavior on different states (hover, active, disabled, loading)
  • Edge cases: zero state, max character limits, very long names, currency formatting edge cases
  • Responsive behavior breakpoints
  • Anything that deviates from the design system — and why
Design QA checklist
  • Font sizes, weights, and spacing match spec
  • Colors use the correct tokens (not hardcoded hex values)
  • Interactive elements have the correct touch targets (≥44px on mobile)
  • Error and loading states are implemented, not just the happy path
  • Dark mode is visually tested on device
  • Text truncation and overflow are handled gracefully
  • Localized strings don't break layout (test with German/Portuguese as stress tests)

Fiat Team
User types
Binance's fiat users span an enormous range of needs, sophistication, and risk profiles. Design for the whole spectrum.
First-time crypto buyers
Most anxious segment. Need the most guidance, the clearest language, and the most reassurance. Every label, every fee line, every KYC prompt needs to work for someone who has never bought crypto before.
High anxiety High guidance needed
Active traders
Need speed above all. They're moving money when the market moves. Every extra step is a conversion lost. Saved payment methods, pre-filled amounts, and one-tap confirm are essential for this segment.
Speed-critical Low friction needed
Arbitrage users
Moving large amounts across platforms quickly. Sensitive to any delay, rate difference, or added friction. They'll abandon immediately if the flow doesn't feel instant and reliable.
Delay-sensitive Large amounts
High-volume / VIP users
Different limit tiers, dedicated support, and sometimes custom payment rails. Design must gracefully handle much larger transaction amounts and the additional compliance requirements that come with them.
Premium tier Enhanced compliance
Context-aware simplicity
The right level of detail and guidance varies dramatically by user type. A first-time buyer needs step-by-step explanation of what a bank transfer reference number is. A VIP trader needs it out of the way. Build systems that adapt — use onboarding state, verification level, and transaction history to calibrate the experience, not a one-size-fits-all approach.

Regulatory & compliance design
Compliance is the invisible architecture of every fiat flow. Knowing the framework by region helps you design with constraints rather than fight them at the end.
The critical mindset shift
On the Fiat team, compliance is not a design afterthought — it's a co-designer. Every jurisdiction has different requirements for disclosures, fee transparency, risk warnings, authentication, and KYC gates. Involve Legal and Compliance at Phase 1 (Discovery), not Phase 5 (Validation). Late compliance reviews cause the most expensive redesigns.

Must-have UI elements for fiat flows (universal)
  • Risk warnings: "Crypto assets are volatile. You could lose your investment." — required in many jurisdictions; design them to be seen, not hidden
  • Fee disclosures: Total cost must be visible before transaction confirmation — always, everywhere
  • Terms & Conditions: Link to relevant ToS with clear indication of what the user is agreeing to
  • GDPR consent: Explicit data consent flows for EU users — design these as part of the flow, not bolted on
  • Sanctions screening: Some users/regions will be blocked — design graceful restriction screens that explain without exposing internal logic
  • AML holds: If a transaction is flagged, design a clear holding state ("We're reviewing your transaction — this can take up to 24 hours") — never leave users in the dark
Compliance as UX — the right framing
Don't treat regulatory requirements as UI clutter to minimize. Fee disclosures, risk warnings, and KYC gates are trust-building moments when designed well. A user who understands exactly what they're agreeing to is more likely to complete a transaction, dispute less, and stay longer. The goal is integration, not concealment.

Fiat Team
KYC & verification UX
KYC is the biggest friction point in fiat onboarding. Our job is to make it feel inevitable, safe, and fast — not punitive.
Common KYC UX failures to avoid
  • Springing KYC requirements mid-transaction — always communicate limits before the user starts a flow
  • Unclear wait times — always show estimated review time (hours, not just "processing")
  • Cryptic rejection messages — state exactly what failed and what the user needs to resubmit
  • Losing form progress on session expiry — save state where technically possible
KYC design best practices
  • Progressive disclosure: unlock KYC steps gradually — don't show all requirements upfront
  • Explain why each piece of information is needed — "We need your address to comply with local regulations"
  • Document upload: show file requirements before upload, not after a failed attempt
  • Show a progress indicator during the verification queue — even a rough one reduces abandonment
  • Celebrate completion — a successful KYC is a big trust moment; mark it clearly

States & annotation checklist
Every screen you design must account for all possible states. Use these checklists before calling any design ready for review or handoff.
States to always design
  • ✅ Default — the baseline state when a user arrives
  • ✅ Hover / Pressed / Focus — interactive feedback for every element
  • ✅ Loading / Skeleton — while data or payment is processing
  • ✅ Empty state — no payment methods added, no transaction history yet
  • ✅ Error state (inline) — field-level validation errors
  • ✅ Error state (toast/banner) — system-level errors (network, server)
  • ✅ Success state — confirmed purchase, successful withdrawal
  • ✅ Disabled — button unavailable until conditions met
  • ✅ Edge cases — very long names, zero amounts, max limits, single-character amounts, overflow text
Annotation checklist — before handoff
  • ☐ Spacing and layout grid documented
  • ☐ Interaction behaviors described (what happens on tap, swipe, long-press)
  • ☐ Transition and animation specs noted (duration, easing curve)
  • ☐ API-dependent content labeled (what's real data vs. placeholder)
  • ☐ Copy strings finalized — or marked TBD with context for copywriter
  • ☐ Accessibility notes added (ARIA labels, focus order, screen reader behavior)
  • ☐ Component variants mapped to the design system (or deviation documented)
  • ☐ Responsive behavior at breakpoints specified
  • ☐ Localization considerations noted (RTL, long strings, currency format)
Fiat-specific states — the ones most designers miss
  • ✅ Pending — extremely common in fiat; bank transfers can stay pending for 1–3 days. Design a full pending state with ETA, reference number, and what to do if it doesn't arrive
  • ✅ Delayed — payment is taking longer than the stated ETA. Proactive communication prevents support contacts — notify users before they contact you
  • ✅ Reversed / Refunded — transaction was cancelled and funds are returning. Show exact return ETA and confirmation that funds are on their way back
  • ✅ Maintenance / Channel offline — payment providers go offline; design graceful "temporarily unavailable" states that suggest an alternative method
  • ✅ KYC-gated — the user needs to verify before proceeding; frame as an unlock, not a wall
  • ✅ Region-blocked — feature unavailable in the user's jurisdiction; explain clearly without exposing legal reasoning
  • ✅ Security badges / SSL indicators on payment screens
  • ✅ Clear fee breakdowns shown before the confirmation step
  • ✅ Rate lock timer visible with countdown
  • ✅ Transaction ID / reference number shown post-confirmation (copyable)
  • ✅ Masked sensitive data by default (card number, bank account)
  • ✅ 2FA prompt for high-value or irreversible actions
  • ✅ Clear cancellation and dispute paths visible after completion
Standards
Accessibility
Binance targets WCAG 2.1 AA compliance. Financial applications have a heightened responsibility for accessibility — errors cost users money.
Color contrast
  • Normal text: minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio
  • Large text (18px+ or 14px+ bold): minimum 3:1
  • UI components and focus states: minimum 3:1
  • Yellow on dark backgrounds passes; yellow on white requires a dark text alternative
  • Never use color alone to convey meaning — always pair with icon or text label
Touch targets
Minimum 44×44px for all interactive elements on mobile. This is a hard requirement, not a guideline. Buttons, list items, checkboxes, and toggle switches must all meet this. Use padding to expand small visual elements to the minimum touch target size.
Screen reader support
  • All form inputs must have visible labels (not just placeholder text)
  • Error messages must be associated with their input via ARIA
  • Dynamic content changes (rate updates, status changes) must use ARIA live regions
  • Amount inputs should announce currency along with the value

Best practices
Hard-won lessons from designing high-stakes financial UX at global scale.
Designing for trust in financial UX ▼
  • Show amounts in the user's local currency first, crypto second — for fiat flows, fiat is primary
  • Use precise numbers, not rounded estimates — "€249.87" not "~€250"
  • Never use patterns that resemble dark patterns — no pre-checked upsells in payment flows
  • Confirmation screens are your last chance to build confidence — make them thorough
  • Processing states must feel alive — a static spinner for a 30-second payment creates anxiety
Designing for global audiences ▼
  • Never assume a banking experience — many Binance users in emerging markets are underbanked
  • Allow extra space in layouts for translated text — German and Portuguese strings are often 30–40% longer than English
  • RTL language support must be considered in every flow — don't design LTR-only
  • Number formatting is locale-specific — always use the device/account locale setting, never hardcode
  • Payment methods vary dramatically by region — design the system, not just the specific method
Working with compliance constraints ▼
  • Compliance is a design partner, not an obstacle — involve them early to avoid late-stage redesigns
  • Regulatory disclosures are required but can be designed well — use progressive disclosure to show required text without burying the user
  • MiCA (EU), FinCEN (US), and local regulations mean some features are region-gated — design for this gracefully with clear explanation
  • KYC requirements change — design the system to accommodate new verification steps without full redesigns
Intentional friction — when to add it ▼
  • In crypto, removing all friction is not always the right goal — for high-value or irreversible actions, a confirmation modal ("Are you sure you want to transfer $10,000?") prevents catastrophic user errors
  • Step-up authentication (2FA prompt) for transactions above a threshold is both a security requirement and a trust signal — design it as reassurance, not an obstacle
  • The key test: does this friction protect the user, or does it just protect the business? If it protects the user, it's worth keeping.
Designing for interruption & resumption ▼
  • Users will leave mid-flow — to find their bank IBAN, check their card number, or authenticate via their banking app during 3DS
  • 3D-Secure authentication is a major interruption: the user is redirected to their bank's app or website and then returns. Design a clear "returning from authentication" holding screen — never drop them back to the start of the flow
  • Bank transfer reference codes: show these prominently and make them copyable — users need to switch apps to paste this into their banking app
  • Save form state wherever technically feasible — a user who re-opens the app should land at the furthest step they reached, not at step 1
  • Edge case: what happens if the user closes the app while a card payment is processing? Design a "pending transaction" state on the home screen that lets them track status without restarting
Avoiding cognitive overload ▼
  • Fiat screens are number-dense by necessity — amounts, fees, rates, limits, ETAs. Be ruthless about hierarchy: the user's amount and final received amount are primary; everything else is secondary
  • Progressive disclosure: show the summary first, details on demand. Don't front-load every piece of information
  • Avoid overloading the payment method selection screen — if a user has 8 saved methods, introduce grouping or a default recommendation rather than a flat undifferentiated list
  • Never show a technical error code to a user. "Error 4012" means nothing — "Your card was declined by your bank. Please try a different card or contact your bank." is actionable
  • Share work early and often — critique is most valuable before you're attached to a direction
  • Critique the design, not the designer — use "this pattern" not "you did"
  • Come to critique with specific questions, not just "what do you think"
  • Document feedback and decisions in Figma comments — design decisions should be traceable
  • Push back with data or principles, not personal preference
